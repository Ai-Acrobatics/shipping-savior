A Foreign Trade Zone is a piece of US soil that, for customs purposes, is treated as if it were outside the country. You can land cargo in an FTZ, store it, repackage it, manipulate it, even use it as raw material in manufacturing — and you don't pay duty until (or unless) the goods leave the zone for US commerce. If they're re-exported, you never pay duty at all.

That's the headline. The reason CFOs care is the math underneath.

## The three savings levers

There are really only three reasons to operate inside an FTZ. Most importers chase one. The smart ones stack at least two.

### 1. Duty deferral

You don't pay duty when goods enter the zone. You pay when they're withdrawn for US consumption. If your inventory turns in 60 days, that's roughly 60 days of duty cash you don't have tied up. On a $20M-a-year import program at a 7.5% blended duty rate, that's about $250k of working capital sitting in your account instead of CBP's. At 8% cost of capital, that's $20k a year — table-stakes savings, but real.

### 2. Weekly entry (the line-item reduction)

Here's the one most people miss. Outside an FTZ, every customs entry has a Merchandise Processing Fee (MPF): 0.3464% of value, but capped at $634.62 per entry. If you file 250 entries a year, you're paying the cap 250 times — about $159k.

Inside an FTZ, you file ONE weekly entry covering everything that left the zone that week. 52 entries instead of 250. At the cap, that's ~$33k. **You just saved ~$126k a year on MPF alone**, before you've touched a single duty calculation.

This is why high-volume LCL importers love FTZs. The duty math might be neutral, but the MPF math is enormous.

### 3. Inverted tariff (the manufacturing one)

If you import a component at a higher duty rate than the finished product it goes into, and you do the manufacturing inside an FTZ, you can elect to pay the lower (finished-good) rate when the product leaves the zone.

The classic case is Chiquita's banana operation in Gulfport, MS. Bananas enter at one rate; once they're cut, packaged, and shipped as finished retail product, they exit at a different (lower) rate. The FTZ election captures that delta legally.

This only works if you actually transform the goods inside the zone. Buying-and-reselling unchanged isn't manufacturing — CBP will look. But assembly, kitting, and processing all qualify.

## When does the math break in your favor?

Rough rule of thumb from the desks I've worked at:

- **Under $5M annual landed cost:** FTZ overhead probably eats the savings. Stay outside.
- **$5M-$20M:** Run the numbers. Weekly entry alone often justifies it if you file 100+ entries a year.
- **$20M+:** Almost always worth it. Duty deferral, MPF cap, and the optionality of inverted tariff stack.
- **You re-export >25% of inventory:** FTZ is a no-brainer at any volume. Re-exported goods never get duty assessed.

## Real-world example

A footwear importer I worked with had:

- $34M annual landed cost
- 7.2% blended duty rate
- 312 customs entries/year (lots of small SKU drops)
- 14% of inventory eventually re-exported to Canada/Mexico

Pre-FTZ: $2.45M duty + $198k MPF + $343k duty on goods that ended up re-exported anyway = $2.99M.

Post-FTZ (weekly entry, drawback on re-exports baked in): $2.10M duty + $33k MPF + $0 on re-exports = $2.13M.

**Annual savings: $860k.** Setup cost was about $80k plus ~$45k/year in zone-operator fees. Payback under 2 months.

## What to do next

1. Pull your last 12 months of customs entries and your blended duty rate. The FTZ Savings Calculator on the platform will do the math.
2. Check FTZ proximity for your primary port — Long Beach, Savannah, Houston, and Port of NY/NJ all have multiple subzones available.
3. If the savings clear $200k/year, talk to a zone operator. Setup is a 90-180 day process and worth scoping early.

The pitch I'd make to your CFO: weekly entry alone usually pays for the operation. Everything else is upside.
