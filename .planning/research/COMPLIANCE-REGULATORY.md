## 1. Record Retention Requirements (5-Year CBP Rule)

### 1.1 Statutory and Regulatory Foundation

**Primary Statute:** [19 USC 1508](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/19/1508) — Recordkeeping (Section 508 of the Tariff Act of 1930, as amended by the Customs Modernization Act of 1993)

**Implementing Regulation:** [19 CFR Part 163](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-19/chapter-I/part-163) — Recordkeeping

**Penalty Statute:** [19 USC 1509](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/19/1509) — Examination of Books and Witnesses

**Key Principle:** Any party involved in importing, exporting, transporting, or storing merchandise in US customs territory must make, keep, and render for examination and inspection all records pertaining to those activities.

### 1.2 Who Must Maintain Records

Under 19 CFR 163.2, the following persons must maintain records:

- **Importers of record** (the party listed on the entry summary)
- **Owners and purchasers** of imported merchandise
- **Consignees** (ultimate consignees)
- **Entry filers** (including customs brokers filing on behalf of importers)
- **Customs brokers** (under both Part 163 and Part 111)
- **Parties transporting or storing bonded merchandise** (carriers, warehouse operators)
- **Foreign Trade Zone operators and users**
- **Drawback claimants and filers**
- **Any person who exports merchandise** (for drawback or other purposes)

### 1.3 The 5-Year Retention Period

**General Rule:** Records must be retained for **5 years from the date of entry** (or filing of a reconciliation, or date of the activity requiring the record).

**Specific Timelines:**

| Record Type | Retention Period | Measured From |
|---|---|---|
| Entry summary and supporting docs | 5 years | Date of entry |
| Reconciliation records | 5 years | Date of filing reconciliation |
| Drawback records | 3 years | Date of liquidation of the drawback claim |
| Export records | 5 years | Date of exportation |
| FTZ records | 5 years | Date of activity (admission, manipulation, removal) |
| Customs broker transaction records | 5 years | Date of entry |
| Powers of attorney (broker) | Duration of POA validity + retention per Part 163 |
| Bond records | 5 years | Date of entry or activity |

**Critical Detail:** The 5-year period is a **maximum** under 19 USC 1508(c)(2) — no retention period may exceed 5 years. However, the statute does not prohibit *voluntary* longer retention. For UFLPA and other compliance purposes, longer retention may be advisable.

### 1.4 The (a)(1)(A) List — Records That Must Be Produced on Demand

The [Appendix to 19 CFR Part 163](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/19/appendix-to_part_163) contains the **Interim (a)(1)(A) List** — the definitive catalog of records that CBP can demand and for which **penalties attach if not produced.**

**Key distinction:**
- **(a)(1)(A) records** = Records required by law/regulation for the entry of merchandise. **Must be maintained and produced upon demand.** Failure to produce triggers penalties under 19 USC 1509.
- **(a)(1)(B) records** = Other records normally kept in the ordinary course of business that pertain to import activity. Must be maintained and made available, but penalty exposure is narrower.

#### Categories on the (a)(1)(A) List

**A. Entry and Entry Summary Records:**
- Entry summary (CBP Form 7501) data elements: entry date, entry type, entry number, entry summary date
- Bond number, type code, surety code
- Port code, importing carrier, mode of transportation
- Country of origin, export date, date of importation
- Importer of record name/number, ultimate consignee name/address
- Description of merchandise, HTSUS number, gross weight, manifest quantity
- Entered value, charges (freight, insurance, packing)
- Relationship between buyer and seller (for valuation purposes)
- Duty/tax/fee amounts, classification information

**B. Invoice Records:**
- Commercial invoice date and number
- Seller/shipper name and address
- Buyer/consignee name and address
- Detailed merchandise description (kind, grade, quantity, weight, marks/numbers)
- Purchase price or value in currency of transaction
- All charges upon the merchandise (packing, freight, insurance)
- All rebates, drawbacks, bounties on exportation
- Country of origin of goods
- All goods or services furnished for production of the merchandise (assists)

**C. Packing Lists:**
- Detailed contents per package/container
- Weights, measurements, marks and numbers

**D. Bills of Lading / Shipping Documents:**
- Ocean/air bill of lading
- Carrier name, vessel/voyage or flight info
- Port of lading and port of discharge
- Description and quantity of freight
- Freight charges

**E. Importer Security Filing (ISF / "10+2") Records:**
- Manufacturer (or supplier) name and address
- Seller name and address
- Buyer name and address
- Ship-to name and address
- Container stuffing location
- Consolidator name and address
- HTSUS number (6-digit minimum)
- Country of origin
- Commodity HTSUS number
- ISF importer and bond information

**F. Classification and Valuation Records:**
- HTS classification determination records (ruling requests, classification analysis)
- Customs valuation records (transaction value worksheets, assist calculations)
- Evidence of transaction value (purchase orders, confirmations, payment records)
- Transfer pricing documentation (related-party transactions)

**G. Bond Records:**
- Continuous bond (CBP Form 301) or single-entry bond
- Bond sufficiency documentation
- Surety information

**H. Power of Attorney:**
- Customs power of attorney (CBP Form 5291)
- Corporate resolutions authorizing signatories

**I. Country of Origin / Marking Records:**
- Documentation supporting country of origin determinations
- NAFTA/USMCA certificates of origin
- Preferential trade program certificates (GSP, CAFTA-DR, etc.)
- Substantial transformation analysis records

**J. Special Category Records:**
- Antidumping/countervailing duty records (AD/CVD)
- Quota/visa records for textiles and apparel
- FDA, USDA, FCC, EPA, CPSC related compliance documents
- UFLPA due diligence documentation (see Section 1.6)

### 1.5 Format Requirements (Electronic vs. Paper)

Under [19 CFR 163.5](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-19/chapter-I/part-163/section-163.5):

**Original Records:**
- Records must be maintained as originals (paper or electronic), **unless** alternative storage methods are adopted per 163.5(b).
- Records created electronically may be maintained in electronic form.

**Alternative Storage Methods (163.5(b)):**
Acceptable formats include but are not limited to:
- Machine-readable data
- CD-ROM / optical media
- Microfiche / microfilm
- Digital imaging systems

**Requirements for Alternative Storage:**
1. **Operational and written procedures** must ensure the imaging/storage process preserves integrity, readability, and security of original records.
2. **Standardized retrieval process** must be documented and functional.
3. **Annual internal testing** of the system must be performed.
4. **Hard-copy reproduction capability** — the recordkeeper must be able to produce (and bear the cost of) paper copies upon CBP demand.
5. **Dual copies required** — one working copy and one backup copy stored in a secure location.
6. **30-day advance written notice** to CBP's Director of Regulatory Audit (Charlotte, NC) before making changes to alternative storage procedures.
7. Records must be **retrievable within a reasonable time** after request.
8. **Adequate safeguards** against alteration, destruction, or deterioration.

**Practical Implication for Digital Platforms:**
- Electronic document management systems are fully acceptable.
- Must maintain both a primary and backup copy (e.g., primary in cloud, backup in separate region/provider).
- Must be able to produce paper printouts on demand.
- Annual testing/validation of retrieval systems is mandatory.
- Any changes to the DMS require 30-day written notice to CBP.

### 1.6 UFLPA Due Diligence Documentation

The **Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act** (P.L. 117-78, effective June 21, 2022) creates a rebuttable presumption that goods from the Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region (XUAR) — or produced by entities on the UFLPA Entity List — are made with forced labor and are prohibited under 19 USC 1307.

**Documentation Requirements for UFLPA:**

To overcome a WRO (Withhold Release Order) or UFLPA detention, importers must provide **"clear and convincing evidence"** including:

- **Supply chain mapping** — full chain from raw material to finished product
- **Commercial transaction records** — invoices, purchase orders at every supply chain tier
- **Financial proof** — payment records, bank transfers, wire confirmations
- **Logistics evidence** — bills of lading, manifests, warehouse receipts tracing physical movement
- **Production evidence** — factory production records, daily production logs, balance of materials reports
- **Due diligence program documentation** — code of conduct, supplier audits, corrective action plans
- **Third-party audit reports** — independent verification of labor conditions

**Retention Implication:** While UFLPA does not specify a separate retention period, all UFLPA due diligence documentation falls within the general 5-year CBP recordkeeping mandate. Given the severity of UFLPA enforcement (goods detained indefinitely until evidence provided), **best practice is to retain UFLPA records for the full 5 years or longer** and ensure immediate retrievability.

### 1.7 FTZ-Specific Record Retention

**Governing Regulation:** [19 CFR Part 146](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-19/chapter-I/part-146) — Foreign Trade Zones

**FTZ Operator Obligations (19 CFR 146.4):**
- The operator supervises and is responsible for all admissions, transfers, removals, recordkeeping, manipulations, manufacturing, destruction, and conditions of storage in the zone.

**Specific FTZ Records:**

| Record Type | Requirement | Reference |
|---|---|---|
| Zone inventory control records | Continuous accountability for all merchandise | 19 CFR 146.4(a), 146.25 |
| Admission records | Document admission of each shipment into FTZ | 19 CFR 146.32-146.40 |
| Manipulation/manufacturing records | Document all processing activities | 19 CFR 146.52-146.53 |
| Status designation records | Privileged foreign (PF) vs. non-privileged foreign (NPF) elections | 19 CFR 146.41-146.42 |
| Removal/transfer records | Document all merchandise leaving the zone | 19 CFR 146.63-146.71 |
| Annual reconciliation report | Must be completed within 90 days of zone year-end | 19 CFR 146.25 |
| Annual reconciliation certification letter | Submitted to CBP port director within 10 working days of reconciliation completion | 19 CFR 146.25 |
| Destruction records | Documentation of merchandise destroyed within zone | 19 CFR 146.52 |
| Weekly entry/exit summaries | Zone log book or electronic equivalent | 19 CFR 146.4 |

**Retention Period for FTZ Records:** 5 years from the date of the activity, consistent with 19 CFR 163. FTZ annual reconciliation reports must be retained for CBP spot checks and audits.

**Critical FTZ Note:** Because FTZ status elections are **irrevocable** (particularly significant after the April 2025 executive order mandating PF status for reciprocal-tariff-scope goods), the records supporting status election decisions should be retained with extreme care and arguably beyond the 5-year minimum.

### 1.8 Penalties for Failure to Maintain Records

**Statute:** [19 USC 1509](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/19/1509)

#### Penalty Tiers

| Violation Level | Maximum Penalty per Release | Cap |
|---|---|---|
| **Willful failure** to maintain, store, or retrieve a demanded record | **$100,000** or **75%** of appraised value of merchandise | Whichever is **less** |
| **Negligent failure** to maintain, store, or retrieve a demanded record | **$10,000** or **40%** of appraised value of merchandise | Whichever is **less** |
| **First violation** (absent willfulness or repeated violations) | Written notice of violation (no monetary penalty) | Warning letter |

**Penalty Calculation Factors:**
- Degree of compliance compared to total number of importations
- Nature of the demanded records
- Recordkeeper's level of cooperation with CBP
- Whether the recordkeeper participated in the Recordkeeping Compliance Program (RCP)

**Additional Consequences:**
- CBP may **reliquidate entries** when records cannot be produced to verify classification, valuation, or origin claims.
- Loss of trade program benefits (GSP, FTZ, drawback) if supporting records are unavailable.
- Potential **19 USC 1592** (fraud/negligence) penalties if missing records conceal material misstatements.
- Revocation or suspension of customs broker license under 19 CFR 111.

### 1.9 CBP Focused Assessments and Audits

**Program:** [CBP Focused Assessment (FA) Program](https://www.cbp.gov/trade/programs-administration/audits/focused-assessment)

A Focused Assessment is a comprehensive audit of an importer's internal controls over import activities to determine compliance risk. Recordkeeping is a **core audit area.**

**FA Process:**
1. **Pre-Assessment Survey** — CBP evaluates the importer's internal control environment, including recordkeeping systems.
2. **Assessment Compliance Testing** — CBP tests actual records against entries to verify accuracy.
3. **Results** — Importer rated as acceptable risk or referred for further action.

**Key Recordkeeping Areas Examined in FAs:**
- Classification records and ruling compliance
- Valuation records (transaction value support, assists, related-party pricing)
- Country of origin documentation
- FTZ inventory and status election records
- Reconciliation records
- ISF filing documentation

**C-TPAT Connection:**

The [Customs-Trade Partnership Against Terrorism (C-TPAT)](https://www.cbp.gov/border-security/ports-entry/cargo-security/CTPAT) Trade Compliance program provides significant benefits related to recordkeeping:

- **Reduced examination rates** — fewer inspections and audits
- **Assignment of a Supply Chain Security Specialist (SCSS)** — dedicated CBP contact
- **Self-audit privilege** — C-TPAT Trade Compliance members can self-audit their compliance function and submit annual reports to CBP in lieu of some audit activities
- **Mutual recognition** — benefits with foreign customs agencies
- **Faster processing** — expedited clearance reduces the window during which documentation must be rapidly accessible

**C-TPAT Trade Compliance members voluntarily self-audit and report annually**, which requires robust internal recordkeeping systems.

### 1.10 Recordkeeping Compliance Program (RCP)

Under [19 CFR 163.12](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-19/chapter-I/part-163/section-163.12):

**Benefits of RCP Certification:**
- Written notice of violation **in lieu of monetary penalty** for first-time, non-willful, non-repeat failures to produce a demanded record.
- Demonstrates "reasonable care" standard to CBP.
- Favorable consideration during Focused Assessments.

**Certification Requirements:**
- Must demonstrate compliance with all applicable recordkeeping laws and regulations.
- CBP considers the size and nature of the importing business and volume of imports.
- Must have documented procedures for record maintenance, retrieval, and production.

**Revocation Risk:**
- Willful failure to produce records → penalties under 163.6(b) **and** removal of certification.
- Repeated failures → removal of certification until corrective action is satisfactory to CBP.

#### Destruction Reminders & Workflow

1. **Approaching expiration alert** — 90 days before 5-year mark, notify compliance officer.
2. **Legal hold check** — Before any destruction, system must verify no active CBP audit, Focused Assessment, litigation hold, or investigation.
3. **Destruction approval workflow** — Require sign-off from compliance officer before records eligible for deletion.
4. **Destruction log** — Maintain an immutable log of what was destroyed, when, and by whom (the destruction log itself should be retained indefinitely).
5. **Never auto-delete (a)(1)(A) records** — Only flag for review; human approval required.

#### Retrieval & Production Requirements

- **Search capability** — Full-text search across all document types by entry number, importer name, HTS code, date range, container number, BOL number.
- **Response time** — CBP expects records within a "reasonable time" upon demand. Industry standard is **30 days** for a summons (19 USC 1509(a)), but Focused Assessments may require faster turnaround.
- **Export formats** — Must produce paper hard copies. Also support PDF, CSV, and structured data exports.
- **Audit trail** — Every access, modification, and export must be logged with timestamp, user, and action.

#### Storage Architecture

- **Primary storage** — Cloud-based (e.g., AWS S3, Azure Blob) with versioning enabled.
- **Backup storage** — Separate geographic region or provider (19 CFR 163.5 requires working copy + backup copy in secure location).
- **Immutability** — Use WORM (Write Once Read Many) storage or object lock for (a)(1)(A) records to prevent alteration.
- **Encryption** — AES-256 at rest, TLS 1.3 in transit.
- **Annual system test** — Document and execute annual retrieval testing per 19 CFR 163.5(b) requirements.

## 2. Data Privacy Implications for Trade Data

### 2.1 Types of Trade Data Handled

A shipping/logistics platform handles multiple categories of sensitive data:

| Data Category | Examples | Sensitivity Level |
|---|---|---|
| **Commercial invoices** | Product descriptions, quantities, unit prices, total values, payment terms, seller/buyer info | HIGH — pricing is trade secret level |
| **Bills of lading** | Shipper, consignee, notify party, cargo description, weights, container numbers, vessel/voyage | MEDIUM — partially public record (see 2.5) |
| **Customs entries** | Entry summaries, HTS classifications, duty amounts, importer of record, bond info | HIGH — confidential with CBP |
| **Financial records** | Duty payments, broker fees, freight charges, insurance costs, bank details | HIGH — financial data |
| **Supplier information** | Factory names, addresses, contact persons, production capabilities | HIGH — competitive intelligence |
| **Pricing data** | Unit costs, landed costs, margin analysis, wholesale/retail pricing | CRITICAL — core trade secrets |
| **Personal data** | Names, addresses, phone numbers, emails of contacts at trading companies | MEDIUM-HIGH — privacy law protected |
| **UFLPA due diligence** | Worker records, audit reports, supply chain maps | HIGH — human rights sensitive |

### 2.2 US Data Privacy Laws Affecting Trade Data

#### 2.2.1 No Federal Comprehensive Privacy Law

As of March 2026, the United States has **no federal comprehensive data privacy law** equivalent to the GDPR. Privacy protections are **sector-specific and state-based.**

**Relevant Federal Laws:**

| Law | Applicability to Trade Data |
|---|---|
| **Trade Secrets Act (18 USC 1905)** | Prohibits federal employees from disclosing trade secrets and confidential commercial information. Governs how CBP treats submitted data. |
| **Privacy Act of 1974 (5 USC 552a)** | Governs federal agency handling of personally identifiable information (PII). Applies to CBP's handling of importer/exporter data. |
| **Freedom of Information Act (5 USC 552)** | Mandates public disclosure of government records, with exemptions for trade secrets (Exemption 4) and personal privacy (Exemptions 6 and 7(C)). |
| **Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (GLBA)** | Applies if the platform handles financial institution data. Limited applicability for most trade platforms. |
| **FTC Act Section 5** | General prohibition on unfair/deceptive practices, including failure to protect consumer data as promised. |

#### 2.2.2 State Privacy Laws (2025-2026 Landscape)

As of early 2026, **20 states have comprehensive privacy laws** on the books, with significant enforcement activity.

**Key State Laws Relevant to Trade Platforms:**

| State | Law | Effective | Key Thresholds |
|---|---|---|---|
| **California** | CPRA (amending CCPA) | Jan 1, 2023 | >$25M revenue, OR data on 100K+ consumers, OR >50% revenue from selling data |
| **Virginia** | VCDPA | Jan 1, 2023 | Process data of 100K+ consumers, OR 25K+ consumers if >50% revenue from data sales |
| **Colorado** | CPA | Jul 1, 2023 | 100K+ consumers/year, OR 25K+ consumers with revenue from data sales |
| **Connecticut** | CTDPA | Jul 1, 2023 | Similar to Virginia thresholds |
| **Indiana** | SB 5 | Jan 1, 2026 | Mirrors Virginia thresholds |
| **Kentucky** | HB 15 | Jan 1, 2026 | Mirrors Virginia thresholds |
| **Rhode Island** | HB 7787 | Jan 1, 2026 | Mirrors Virginia thresholds |

**Applicability to a Trade Platform:**
- If the platform is **California-based** or has significant California users, **CCPA/CPRA applies.**
- B2B exception: CCPA originally exempted B2B data, but this exemption **expired January 1, 2023.** Employee and B2B personal information is now covered.
- Trade platform handling personal data of contacts at importing/exporting companies (names, emails, phone numbers) is likely subject to CCPA if thresholds are met.
- **Key rights under CCPA/CPRA:** Right to know, right to delete, right to correct, right to opt-out of sale/sharing, right to limit use of sensitive personal information.

#### 2.2.3 Confidential Business Information (CBI) with CBP

CBP treats submitted commercial information as confidential under [19 CFR Part 103](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-19/chapter-I/part-103) and the Trade Secrets Act (18 USC 1905):

**What CBP Keeps Confidential:**
- Entry summary data (classification, valuation, duty amounts)
- Commercial invoice details
- Importer identification and transaction data
- Customs broker records
- Bond information
- UFLPA due diligence submissions

**What May Be Publicly Disclosed (with limitations):**
- Inbound vessel manifest data (shipper, consignee, description, weight) — public under 19 USC 1431
- BUT: importers/consignees can request **manifest confidentiality** under 19 CFR 103.31 to suppress their name and address (valid for 2 years, renewable)

**FOIA Exemptions Protecting Trade Data:**
- **Exemption 4** — Trade secrets and confidential commercial/financial information
- **Exemption 6** — Personal privacy (names, addresses of individuals)
- **Exemption 7(C)** — Law enforcement privacy (investigation records)

### 2.3 GDPR Implications for EU Trading Partners

#### 2.3.1 When GDPR Applies to a US Trade Platform

The GDPR ([Regulation (EU) 2016/679](https://gdpr-info.eu/)) applies to a US-based shipping/logistics platform when:

1. **The platform has an establishment in the EU** (office, subsidiary, server infrastructure).
2. **The platform processes personal data of individuals in the EU** in the context of offering goods/services to them (regardless of payment).
3. **The platform monitors the behavior** of individuals in the EU.

**Trade-Specific Triggers:**
- Processing contact information (names, emails, phone numbers) of employees at EU-based suppliers, manufacturers, or freight forwarders.
- Handling shipping documents that identify individuals at EU companies.
- Receiving data from EU customs authorities or trade partners.
- Marketing the platform to EU-based importers/exporters.

**Note:** GDPR protects **natural persons only**, not companies. However, contact persons at companies (named individuals on invoices, BOLs, emails) **are** protected data subjects.

#### 2.3.2 Core GDPR Principles Affecting Trade Data

| Principle | Article | Impact on Trade Platform |
|---|---|---|
| **Lawfulness, fairness, transparency** | Art. 5(1)(a) | Must have legal basis for processing. Likely bases: contract performance, legitimate interest, or legal obligation (customs compliance). |
| **Purpose limitation** | Art. 5(1)(b) | Data collected for customs compliance cannot be repurposed for marketing or analytics without separate legal basis. |
| **Data minimization** | Art. 5(1)(c) | Collect only what is necessary. Don't store unnecessary personal details from trade documents. |
| **Accuracy** | Art. 5(1)(d) | Keep trade partner data current; provide mechanism for correction. |
| **Storage limitation** | Art. 5(1)(e) | **Key tension:** GDPR says don't keep data longer than necessary. CBP requires 5-year retention. Resolution: The 5-year retention is justified by **legal obligation** (Art. 6(1)(c)) and the GDPR explicitly recognizes that regulatory retention requirements are a valid basis for extended storage. Document this in the data retention policy. |
| **Integrity and confidentiality** | Art. 5(1)(f) | Appropriate security measures required (encryption, access controls). |

#### 2.3.3 Cross-Border Transfer Mechanisms

Transferring personal data of EU individuals to a US-based platform requires one of the following:

1. **EU-US Data Privacy Framework (DPF)** — Adequacy decision adopted July 10, 2023 (Commission Implementing Decision (EU) 2023/1795). US organizations that self-certify to the DPF can receive EU personal data. **Recommended approach for trade platforms.**

2. **Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)** — Pre-approved contractual terms adopted by the European Commission (June 2021 version). Must be supplemented with a Transfer Impact Assessment (TIA). **Fallback if DPF is challenged or unavailable.**

3. **Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs)** — For intra-group transfers. Typically relevant for large multinationals, not SaaS platforms.

4. **Derogations (Art. 49)** — Limited exceptions for occasional transfers:
   - Explicit consent of the data subject
   - Necessary for performance of a contract
   - Necessary for important reasons of public interest
   - **Note:** These derogations should not be relied upon for systematic, recurring transfers.

**Practical Recommendation:** Self-certify under the EU-US DPF and maintain SCCs as a backup mechanism.

#### 2.3.4 Data Protection Officer (DPO) Requirement

Under GDPR Article 37, a DPO must be appointed when:

- Core activities require **regular and systematic monitoring of data subjects on a large scale.**
- Core activities involve **large-scale processing of special categories of data.**

**For a trade platform:**
- Processing contact details of trade partners is unlikely to constitute "regular and systematic monitoring" on its own.
- However, if the platform processes **UFLPA due diligence data** that includes worker information, health/safety audit data, or similar sensitive information about identified EU persons, DPO appointment may be triggered.
- **Recommended:** Even if not legally required, appoint a DPO or privacy lead as a best practice. This signals compliance maturity to EU trading partners.

### 2.4 Data Security Requirements

#### 2.4.1 C-TPAT Cybersecurity Criteria

The [C-TPAT Minimum Security Criteria](https://www.cbp.gov/border-security/ports-entry/cargo-security/ctpat-customs-trade-partnership-against-terrorism/apply/security-criteria) include **13 cybersecurity requirements** applicable across all 12 C-TPAT eligibility categories:

1. **Written cybersecurity policies** — based on NIST Cybersecurity Framework or equivalent industry standard.
2. **Software/hardware protections** — antivirus, firewalls, intrusion detection/prevention systems.
3. **Data backup and protection** — minimum weekly backups; consult NIST standards for media sanitization/destruction.
4. **Infrastructure testing** — regular vulnerability assessments and penetration testing; corrective action for findings.
5. **Threat reporting** — policies for reporting threats to government entities and business partners.
6. **Unauthorized access monitoring** — systems to detect unauthorized access to IT systems/data and abuse of policies.
7. **Annual review** — cybersecurity policies must be reviewed and updated at least annually.
8. **Access control** — restrict access based on job roles/responsibilities (principle of least privilege).
9. **Incident response plan** — documented procedures for responding to cybersecurity incidents.
10. **Training and awareness** — cybersecurity training for employees with access to IT systems.
11. **Physical security** — protect IT infrastructure from unauthorized physical access.
12. **Third-party risk management** — assess security posture of service providers and business partners.
13. **Account management** — procedures for creating, modifying, and deactivating user accounts.

**NIST Framework Alignment:** C-TPAT encourages alignment with the [NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)](https://www.nist.gov/cyberframework): Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover.

#### 2.4.2 PCI DSS (Payment Card Data)

If the platform processes, stores, or transmits payment card data (e.g., for duty payments, freight charges, subscription billing):

**PCI DSS 4.0** (mandatory since April 1, 2025) requires:
- Multi-factor authentication (MFA) for all access to the cardholder data environment (CDE).
- Continuous monitoring (not just annual checks).
- Targeted risk analysis to define security control frequencies.
- Strong cryptography for transmission and storage of cardholder data.

**Practical Approach:** **Do not store card data.** Use a PCI-compliant payment processor (Stripe, Braintree, etc.) and tokenize. This limits PCI scope to SAQ-A (minimal requirements).

#### 2.4.3 Customs Broker Cybersecurity (19 CFR 111)

Under the [modernized broker regulations](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-19/chapter-I/part-111) (effective December 19, 2022):

**Breach Notification (19 CFR 111.22):**
- Customs brokers must notify the **CBP Security Operations Center (SOC)** at cbpsoc@cbp.dhs.gov within **72 hours** of discovering a breach of electronic or physical records relating to customs business.
- Within **10 business days** of the initial notification, provide an updated list of any additional known compromised importer identification numbers.
- Additional information subsequently discovered must be reported within **72 hours** of that discovery.

**Recordkeeping Contact (19 CFR 111.21):**
- Each broker must designate a knowledgeable employee as the party responsible for brokerage-wide recordkeeping requirements.

**Records within US Customs Territory:**
- All originals of records (including electronic formats) must be maintained **within the customs territory of the United States.**

### 2.5 CBP Data Sharing and Public Availability

#### 2.5.1 What Trade Data Is Public vs. Confidential

| Data Type | Public/Confidential | Legal Basis | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Inbound vessel manifest (BOL) data** | **Public** (by default) | 19 USC 1431, 46 USC 60105, 19 CFR 103.31 | Shipper, consignee, description, weight, container numbers. Only vessel shipments — air/rail/truck manifests are NOT public. |
| **Entry summary data** | **Confidential** | Trade Secrets Act, FOIA Exemption 4 | Classification, valuation, duty amounts — not publicly available. |
| **Importer/consignee identity on manifests** | **Public unless confidentiality requested** | 19 CFR 103.31 | Manifest confidentiality must be applied for; valid 2 years; covers all ports. |
| **Export data (AES/EEI)** | **Confidential** | 13 USC 301(g), 15 CFR 30.60 | Electronic Export Information filed through AES is confidential and exempt from public disclosure. |
| **Census Bureau import statistics** | **Confidential (source data)** | 19 CFR 103, Census confidentiality provisions | Statistical copies of entries held by Census are confidential; aggregate statistics are published. |
| **AD/CVD case data** | **Partially public** | Commerce Department proceedings | Case filings and determinations are public; proprietary business information (PBI) is protected. |
| **ITRAC (Importer Trade Activity) data** | **Available to importer of record** | FOIA | Importers can request their own data from CBP for $250-$300 processing fee. |

**Critical Insight for Platform Design:**
- **BOL data is public.** Third-party data providers (ImportGenius, Panjiva/S&P Global, Descartes) already aggregate and sell this data. Your platform's users should be aware that their vessel manifest data is accessible to competitors unless confidentiality is requested.
- **Entry summary data is protected.** This is the most commercially sensitive customs data (values, classifications, duty amounts) and is NOT publicly available.
- **Manifest confidentiality is opt-in** and must be renewed every 2 years.

#### 2.5.2 Census Bureau Treatment of Export Data

Under [15 CFR 30.60](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-15/subtitle-B/chapter-I/part-30/subpart-G/section-30.60):

- Electronic Export Information (EEI) collected via AES is **confidential under 13 USC 301(g).**
- May only be used for official purposes as authorized by the Secretary of Commerce.
- Exempt from public disclosure unless the Secretary determines otherwise.
- Individual export transactions are **never published**; only aggregate statistics.

### 2.6 Implementation Notes for a Trade Data Platform

#### 2.6.1 Data Classification Scheme

Implement a four-tier classification:

| Level | Label | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **L1** | **PUBLIC** | Freely available or already in public domain | Published vessel manifest data, aggregate trade statistics, HTS tariff schedule |
| **L2** | **INTERNAL** | Not sensitive but not for public release | Platform analytics, non-PII operational data, carrier schedule data |
| **L3** | **CONFIDENTIAL** | Business-sensitive; unauthorized access causes harm | Entry summary data, commercial invoices, classification records, supplier details, contact PII |
| **L4** | **RESTRICTED** | Highly sensitive; unauthorized access causes severe harm | Pricing/cost data, financial account details, UFLPA due diligence, payment card data (if any), supplier margin analysis |

#### 2.6.2 Encryption Requirements

| Scope | Standard | Implementation |
|---|---|---|
| **Data at rest** | AES-256 | Encrypt all L3 and L4 data. Use server-side encryption (SSE) with customer-managed keys (CMK) for multi-tenant separation. |
| **Data in transit** | TLS 1.3 | All API calls, webhooks, browser connections. Enforce HSTS. Disable TLS 1.0/1.1. |
| **Database field-level** | AES-256 with column encryption | Encrypt pricing data, bank details, importer identification numbers at the field level. |
| **Backup encryption** | AES-256 | All backup copies encrypted, including the required 19 CFR 163.5 backup copy. |
| **Key management** | AWS KMS / Azure Key Vault / GCP Cloud KMS | Per-tenant encryption keys for multi-tenant deployments. Rotate keys annually. |

#### 2.6.3 Access Controls

```
Access Control Model: Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) + Attribute-Based Access Control (ABAC)

Roles:
├── Platform Admin
│   └── Full system access (no access to tenant trade data without audit)
├── Tenant Admin (Importer/Broker)
│   └── Full access to own organization's data only
├── Compliance Officer
│   └── Read access to all records within their organization
│   └── Approve/deny destruction requests
│   └── View audit logs
├── Operations User
│   └── Read/write access to entries, shipments, and documents
│   └── No access to pricing/financial records unless explicitly granted
├── Viewer
│   └── Read-only access to assigned shipments/entries
└── CBP Auditor (temporary, scoped)
    └── Read-only access to specific records demanded by CBP
    └── Time-limited access with full audit logging

ABAC Attributes:
- tenant_id (MANDATORY on every query — prevents cross-tenant data leakage)
- data_classification_level (L1-L4)
- record_category ((a)(1)(A) vs. (a)(1)(B))
- time_sensitivity (active, nearing_expiration, expired_pending_destruction)
```

#### 2.6.4 Audit Logging

All of the following events must be logged immutably:

- **Authentication events** — login, logout, failed login, MFA challenges
- **Data access** — who viewed which record, when, from what IP
- **Data modification** — before/after values, who changed, when
- **Data export** — what records exported, format, destination, who requested
- **Destruction events** — what was destroyed, approval chain, timestamp
- **CBP production events** — when records were produced to CBP, what was included
- **Privilege changes** — role assignments, permission grants/revocations
- **Anomalous access** — access outside business hours, bulk downloads, cross-tenant queries

**Log Retention:** Audit logs should be retained for at least 7 years (exceeding the 5-year CBP requirement to cover the audit trail of records throughout their lifecycle).

**Log Integrity:** Use append-only storage with cryptographic hash chaining or a dedicated SIEM solution.

#### 2.6.5 Balancing 5-Year CBP Retention with GDPR Data Minimization

This is the core tension in trade data privacy:

**The Conflict:**
- CBP requires 5-year retention of all entry records (19 USC 1508).
- GDPR requires storage limitation — don't keep data longer than necessary (Art. 5(1)(e)).
- Some trade documents contain personal data of EU individuals (contact names on invoices, BOLs).

**The Resolution:**

1. **Legal obligation basis (Art. 6(1)(c)):** GDPR explicitly recognizes that processing (including retention) is lawful when necessary for compliance with a legal obligation. The 5-year CBP requirement **is** such an obligation. Document this in your Record of Processing Activities (ROPA) and privacy policy.

2. **Implement data lifecycle management:**
   - During the 5-year retention period: retain full records as required by CBP.
   - At 5-year mark: if no GDPR-covered personal data, destroy per retention policy.
   - If GDPR-covered personal data exists: **pseudonymize** or **anonymize** personal identifiers if the records are no longer needed for customs purposes. If the records must be retained in full for potential audits, document the legal obligation justification.

3. **Minimize at collection:**
   - Don't collect unnecessary personal data from EU contacts beyond what appears on trade documents.
   - Don't enrich trade data with additional EU personal data unless necessary.

4. **Data subject rights during retention:**
   - **Right to erasure (Art. 17):** Can be refused during the 5-year retention period if retention is necessary for compliance with a legal obligation (Art. 17(3)(b)).
   - **Right to restriction of processing (Art. 18):** Data subjects can request restriction, but data can still be stored (just not actively processed) and can still be produced to CBP on demand.
   - **Right of access (Art. 15):** Must be honored — data subjects can request copies of their personal data held in trade records.

5. **Transparency:** Privacy notice must clearly explain:
   - What personal data is collected from trade documents
   - That data is retained for 5 years pursuant to US customs law
   - How to exercise GDPR rights
   - Cross-border transfer mechanisms used

#### 2.6.6 Handling Supplier Pricing Data

Supplier pricing data (unit costs, FOB prices, landed costs, margin analysis) is the **most commercially sensitive** data in any trade platform.

**Protection Requirements:**
- **Classification:** L4 (Restricted) — highest protection tier.
- **Field-level encryption** in database — even database administrators should not see plaintext pricing in production.
- **Access restricted** to named individuals with explicit grant (not just role-based).
- **Audit every access** — log who viewed pricing data, when, and for what purpose.
- **No inclusion in bulk exports** — pricing data excluded from standard data exports; requires separate authorization.
- **Separate from other trade data in backups** — if possible, use separate encryption keys for pricing data.
- **Contractual protections** — platform terms must prohibit sharing/selling pricing data and must specify how pricing data is protected.
- **Aggregation controls** — if the platform provides analytics, ensure that individual supplier pricing cannot be reverse-engineered from aggregate statistics.

#### 2.6.7 Multi-Tenant Data Isolation

For a platform serving multiple importers, customs brokers, or trading companies:

**Architecture Options:**

| Model | Isolation Level | Cost | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Silo (separate DB per tenant)** | Highest | Highest | Recommended for large enterprise importers with strict CBI requirements |
| **Bridge (shared DB, separate schemas)** | High | Medium | Good balance for mid-market; each tenant's data in its own PostgreSQL schema |
| **Pool (shared DB, shared tables, tenant_id column)** | Lowest | Lowest | Acceptable for SMB with strong row-level security (RLS) |

**Mandatory Controls Regardless of Model:**

1. **Tenant context in every query** — enforce `tenant_id` at the ORM/middleware level. Never trust client-supplied tenant context alone.
2. **Row-Level Security (RLS)** — if using pool model, enable PostgreSQL RLS policies that filter by `tenant_id` on every table.
3. **API-level tenant validation** — every API request must authenticate and authorize the tenant context before any data access.
4. **Cross-tenant query prevention** — no user (including platform admins) should be able to query across tenants without explicit, logged authorization.
5. **Tenant-specific encryption keys** — use per-tenant keys for L3/L4 data so that a key compromise affects only one tenant.
6. **Backup isolation** — ensure that tenant-level restore is possible without exposing other tenants' data.
7. **Penetration testing** — specifically test for Insecure Direct Object Reference (IDOR) vulnerabilities that could expose cross-tenant data.
8. **Data export isolation** — CBP may demand records from one importer; the production must not inadvertently include another tenant's data.
9. **Deletion isolation** — if a tenant leaves the platform, their data must be completely removable (subject to the 5-year retention obligation — data cannot be deleted before the retention period expires even if the tenant departs).

**Trade-Specific Multi-Tenant Considerations:**
- A customs broker tenant may manage data for dozens of importers — implement sub-tenant isolation so that an importer's data within a broker's account is isolated from other importers.
- If the platform aggregates BOL data across tenants for analytics, ensure that competitive pricing and supplier data cannot leak between tenants.
- C-TPAT requires that supply chain partners' data is protected — a multi-tenant breach could compromise multiple C-TPAT members simultaneously.
